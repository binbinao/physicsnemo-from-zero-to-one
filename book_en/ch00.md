## 0.1 A World Being Rewritten by AI: From AlphaFold to GraphCast

If I had to summarize the scientific transformation of 2020–2026 in one sentence, it would be:

> **Scientists have begun to accept something—AI is not just a tool for writing code; it can do science itself.**

Let's look back at a few landmark moments:

- **2020**: DeepMind released AlphaFold2, turning protein structure prediction from "one Nature paper every few years" into "a new structure every few hours." A 50-year-old structural biology challenge was leveled overnight.
- **2021**: Karniadakis proposed the unified framework of "Physics-Informed Machine Learning" (PIML) in *Nature Reviews Physics*, marking AI4Science's elevation from "scattered tools" to a "research paradigm."
- **2022**: NVIDIA Modulus (the predecessor of PhysicsNeMo) went open-source, packaging methods like PINNs, neural operators, and diffusion models—previously scattered across papers—**into an SDK that could run on industrial GPU clusters** for the first time.
- **2023**: DeepMind released GraphCast, compressing global weather forecasting from "supercomputers running for hours" to "a single GPU running for 1 minute," with prediction accuracy **surpassing** the European Centre for Medium-Range Weather Forecasts (ECMWF)'s traditional numerical methods.
- **2024–2026**: NVIDIA renamed Modulus to PhysicsNeMo, aligning it with the NeMo framework family (NeMo is the umbrella name for large models/speech/vision). The product logic behind this rename is clear—**the product weight of physics simulation inside NVIDIA is now on par with large language models**.

![F0.1 AI4S Key Events Timeline (2020–2026)](assets/f0_1_ai4s_timeline.png)

This is an accelerating curve. **And it's still accelerating**—if you don't get on board, the gap widens every six months.

But there's another side to this story, one that anyone who has done traditional numerical simulation knows well: **pain**.

## 0.2 Three Barriers of Traditional Numerical Simulation

Let me tell a real story first.

An NVH (Noise, Vibration, and Harshness) team at an automotive OEM needed to run a suspension tuning parameter sweep—50 parameter combinations, each requiring a full ANSYS Mechanical modal analysis. One simulation takes 45 minutes; with pre/post-processing it's 1 hour; each parameter set takes 1.75 hours. Running 50 parameters sequentially: **87.5 hours**—an engineer's entire two weeks with nothing else getting done.

Every CAE engineer has experienced this. The bottleneck isn't the solver (solvers have been optimized by ANSYS / Abaqus / STAR-CCM+ for 30 years); **the bottleneck is three barriers**:

### 0.2.1 Barrier 1: Meshing

Every geometry change requires re-meshing. A complex model takes 4 hours to mesh; one mistake and you start over. A CFD engineer probably spends a third of their career meshing.

### 0.2.2 Barrier 2: Solving

For nonlinear, transient, multi-physics coupled problems, the solver must iterate to convergence. A full-vehicle CFD single-condition run of 8–24 hours is normal. GPU acceleration helps somewhat, but the inherent complexity of the physics cannot be compressed.

### 0.2.3 Barrier 3: Parameter Sweep

Engineering isn't about solving one condition—engineering is about **finding the optimum**. That means sweeping tens to hundreds of parameter combinations. Barriers 1 + 2 reappear for every combination. **One week becomes one year; one year becomes a career**.

![F0.2 Traditional CFD vs. AI Surrogate Model Work-Hours Comparison](assets/f0_2_cfd_vs_ai_comparison.png)

> **AI is not here to replace solvers—it's here to conquer these three barriers.**

Specifically:

- **Barrier 1 (Meshing)**: Neural operators can learn on **already-discretized** representations (structured grids, voxels, point clouds, etc.); training data typically still comes from **already-meshed CFD/FEM**. It's not "no mesh at all," but rather that the **inference stage** doesn't require re-meshing and re-solving every time.
- **Barrier 2 (Solving)**: A trained surrogate model infers in ≤ 1 second, **compressing 8 hours into 0.5 seconds**—it's not a 16,000× speedup per se; it's 16,000× of engineer waiting time eliminated.
- **Barrier 3 (Parameter Sweep)**: Neural operators learn the mapping from "input function → output function," **one model solves all conditions**—50 parameters? Inferred in 2 seconds.

This is the real value of AI4Science from an engineering perspective. **It doesn't make CAE engineers obsolete; it frees them from "waiting for simulations" so they can actually do engineering design.**

## 0.3 What PhysicsNeMo Is—and What It Is Not

With the background covered, let's return to the protagonist: **PhysicsNeMo**.

### 0.3.1 One-Sentence Definition

> PhysicsNeMo is **NVIDIA's open-source, industrial-grade AI physics simulation SDK** that packages methods like PINNs, neural operators, diffusion models, and distributed training—previously scattered across papers—into composable, extensible, deployable Python modules.

**Key terms explained**:
- **Industrial-grade**: Not a research demo—designed to run in production environments, supporting multi-GPU distributed training, checkpointing, and ONNX/Triton inference deployment.
- **SDK**: You're not here to "use a product"—you're here to "compose a set of capabilities" into your own solution.
- **Open-source**: Apache-2.0; code, documentation, and examples are all on GitHub.

### 0.3.2 Its Dual-Framework Architecture

PhysicsNeMo is actually **two repositories combined**:

```
NVIDIA/physicsnemo (main framework)
├─ Neural operators (FNO / AFNO / DeepONet / GNO)
├─ Large model architectures (FourCastNet / GraphCast replications)
├─ Distributed training (DDP / FSDP / model parallelism)
└─ Deployment tools (ONNX / Triton integration)

NVIDIA/physicsnemo-sym (formerly Modulus-Sym)
├─ PINN declarative API
├─ Symbolic PDE definitions (SymPy-based)
├─ CSG geometry and samplers
└─ Hydra config-driven Solver
```

This book's design follows this structure: **Chapters 0–3 use `physicsnemo-sym` (PINN first-principles), and from Chapter 4 onward we switch to the `physicsnemo` main framework (neural operators + large models + distributed)**.

### 0.3.3 What It Is **Not**

To make sure you don't buy the wrong ticket, I need to be clear about what PhysicsNeMo is not:

| It is **not** | The reality |
|---|---|
| A GUI tool | It's a Python SDK, entirely code-driven |
| A replacement for ANSYS / STAR-CCM+ | It's a "surrogate model training framework" that **still needs traditional solvers to generate training data** |
| A tune-and-run "AutoML for Physics" | PINN loss weights, network architectures, and sampling strategies all require engineer tuning |
| Only friendly to NVIDIA GPUs | The primary backend is PyTorch + CUDA; CPU works too (just slower) |
| A stable, unchanging API | v2.0 was just released in 2026-05 as a major refactor; the API is still evolving |

### 0.3.4 Comparison with Similar Projects

| Framework | Positioning | Strengths | Weaknesses |
|---|---|---|---|
| **NVIDIA PhysicsNeMo** | Industrial-grade AI physics SDK | Most complete industrial features, strongest documentation ecosystem, deep integration with NVIDIA GPU/Triton | Steep learning curve; v2.0 API still evolving |
| DeepXDE | Academic PINN library | Fastest to get started, clear documentation | Weak industrial deployment capabilities; no large models/distributed |
| JAX-PINN / Pytorch-PINN | Roll your own | Flexible, controllable | You have to write everything an SDK should provide |
| FEniCS + ML | Traditional FEM plus AI | High numerical accuracy | AI integration is "bolted on," not native |
| SimNet (discontinued) | NVIDIA's previous generation | — | Superseded by Modulus → PhysicsNeMo |

> **Conclusion**: If you're working on industrial problems, PhysicsNeMo is virtually the only "industrial-grade" option available today. If you're just writing an academic paper, DeepXDE will suffice. This book chose PhysicsNeMo because our target readers want **solutions that can actually be deployed in production**.

## 0.6 Some Honest Words

While writing this book, I kept asking myself one question—**why should a reader spend 6 weeks learning an open-source framework whose v2.0 just launched and whose API is still evolving?**

I have three answers:

**First**, it's the trend. AI4Science is not a question of "will it happen"—it's a question of "when will it happen to you." Learning half a year earlier means you can become the person on your team who **can use AI to compress simulation work-hours to 1%**—whether you call this role "AI4S engineer" or "CAE engineer who can use AI."

**Second**, learning an industrial-grade framework has **transferable value**. PhysicsNeMo v2.0's API will change, but the PINN three-part loss, the Fourier spectral methods of neural operators, the parallelism strategies of distributed training—these underlying concepts **won't change for a decade**. You're learning the "mother tongue" of physics + AI; the tool is just one "dialect."

**Third**, from my perspective as a cloud vendor solutions practitioner, the reality I see is—**a great many enterprises are asking "how can AI help us with simulation," but there are far too few people in the market who can actually deliver**. This is a direction with a massive supply-demand gap. Getting in early pays off.

One final word: **this book will not tell you AI is omnipotent—quite the opposite. In every chapter I specifically list "failure cases" and "pitfall logs."** I hope that after reading this, you'll be able to distinguish what **can be deployed today**, what **still needs 2 more years**, and what is **a research demo that will never make it to production**.
